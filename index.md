**Key features of the pattern:**
- Both remissions lasted approximately 2–3 weeks
- Both ended with return to the same baseline high-intensity pain
- Neither is convincingly linked to any treatment or intervention
- The December remission was spontaneous; the March remission followed surgery but the surgeons "didn't do much that could improve things"
- No other remissions or significant relief periods exist in the entire 10-month record

**What this pattern suggests:**
- An underlying process that waxes and wanes on a multi-week timescale
- The baseline state is active pain; remission is the exception
- The process fully resets — pain character and severity return identically each time
- This argues against static structural causes (which don't spontaneously remit) and against progressive degeneration (which doesn't reset cleanly)
- This argues toward episodic inflammatory, mechanical (intermittent), or cyclical processes

### The Pain Spread

Pain began at the three laparoscopic incision sites (right lower quadrant area), then spread to:
- Midline abdomen
- Upper right quadrant
- Pelvic region

This anatomical spread beyond the surgical field is significant. It could indicate:
- Central sensitization (nervous system amplifying pain signals beyond the original site)
- A process not confined to the surgical site (e.g., diffuse peritoneal inflammation, visceral hypersensitivity)
- Referred pain from a single source along shared nerve pathways

The March 2026 remission is particularly puzzling: the surgeons found nothing to fix and "didn't do much," yet she became pain-free for 2–3 weeks. Possible explanations:

### 9.1 Crohn's Disease (Ileocecal)

**Estimated Probability: HIGH**

**Confirmatory Examinations:**
- Fecal calprotectin (elevated during flare)
- CRP / ESR during active pain episode
- MR enterography (MRI of the small bowel — gold standard for ileocecal Crohn's)
- Capsule endoscopy (if MR enterography is inconclusive)
- **Review of resected appendix/cecal pole pathology** — presence of granulomas would be near-diagnostic
- Repeat ileocolonoscopy with biopsies of the neo-terminal ileum
- Anti-Saccharomyces cerevisiae antibodies (ASCA) and perinuclear anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (p-ANCA)

**What It Is:**
Crohn's disease is a chronic inflammatory bowel disease that can affect any part of the gastrointestinal tract but most commonly involves the terminal ileum and cecum (the ileocecal region). Unlike ulcerative colitis, which affects only the colon's inner lining, Crohn's inflammation is transmural (extends through the full thickness of the bowel wall) and can be patchy, with affected segments separated by normal-appearing bowel ("skip lesions"). The disease follows a relapsing-remitting course, with flares of active inflammation alternating with periods of quiescence. It is most commonly diagnosed in young adults (20s–30s) and has its highest prevalence in Northern European populations.

The hallmarks of Crohn's disease include abdominal pain (often right-sided, mimicking appendicitis), fatigue, and variable bowel symptoms — though bowel habits can remain deceptively normal in limited disease. Diagnosis requires a combination of endoscopy with biopsy, imaging, and laboratory markers. The presence of non-caseating granulomas on biopsy is highly characteristic. The disease is driven by a dysregulated immune response to gut microbiota in genetically susceptible individuals, with both innate and adaptive immune pathways involved.

**Why It Fits This Case:**
Crohn's disease involving the ileocecal region is one of the most common mimics of appendicitis, and Anna's history of months of "subacute appendicitis" requiring repeated antibiotic courses before surgery is a recognized presentation pattern for Crohn's. The fact that a cecal pole resection was needed (suggesting inflammation extending into the cecal wall) further supports this. Critically, the relapsing-remitting pain pattern — two clear remissions of 2–3 weeks followed by full return to baseline — is the hallmark disease course of Crohn's. Her Northern European ancestry places her in the highest-prevalence population. Prior colonoscopy could have missed it if the affected segment was already resected, and "normal digestion" does not exclude limited ileocecal disease. The most decisive piece of evidence — the pathology report from the resected cecal pole — has not yet been reviewed in this investigation.

**Note on differential with parasitic infection:** Both ileocecal Crohn's and certain parasitic infections (particularly Strongyloides, amebiasis) can cause ileocecal inflammation mimicking appendicitis. Anna's prior residence in Dubai adds a parasitic exposure dimension. The pathology report is critical for distinguishing these: Crohn's shows non-caseating granulomas; parasitic infection may show eosinophilic infiltration, larvae, or organism-specific patterns. Both should be considered when reviewing the histology.

### 9.2 Post-Surgical Chronic Pain Syndrome with Central Sensitization

**Estimated Probability: HIGH**

**Confirmatory Examinations:**
- Quantitative sensory testing (QST) — measures pain thresholds and detects central sensitization
- Diagnostic nerve block (ilioinguinal / iliohypogastric) — if pain temporarily resolves, confirms peripheral nerve involvement feeding central sensitization
- Conditioned pain modulation (CPM) testing — assesses descending pain inhibition
- Trial of centrally-acting agents: pregabalin, gabapentin, or duloxetine (therapeutic trial as diagnostic tool)
- Brain/spinal cord functional MRI (research settings only)
- Pain catastrophizing scale and central sensitization inventory (validated questionnaires)

**What It Is:**
Chronic post-surgical pain (CPSP) is defined as pain that develops or increases in intensity after a surgical procedure, persists beyond the expected healing period (typically >3 months), and cannot be explained by other causes such as infection or ongoing disease. It affects 10–50% of patients after common surgeries, with abdominal procedures carrying moderate risk. Central sensitization is a neurophysiological state where the central nervous system amplifies pain signals — neurons in the spinal cord and brain become hyperexcitable, lowering pain thresholds and expanding the area of perceived pain beyond the original injury site. This can explain why pain spreads from incision sites to anatomically distant regions.

Central sensitization can be triggered by prolonged peripheral nociceptive input (e.g., months of pre-surgical pain from repeated appendicitis episodes) and maintained long after the original tissue damage has resolved. The nervous system essentially "learns" to be in pain. Risk factors include prolonged pre-operative pain (Anna had 7+ months), younger age, female sex, and psychological distress. Importantly, central sensitization can produce episodic fluctuations because the threshold for pain expression can shift with sleep, stress, hormonal cycles, and other modulators — though clean 2–3 week remissions are less typical of pure central sensitization.

**Why It Fits This Case:**
Anna had 7 months of recurrent pain before surgery, followed by a "complicated" procedure, followed by pain that progressively spread from incision sites to midline and upper right quadrant — a classic central sensitization spread pattern. The extensive diagnostic workup showing no structural cause is consistent with CPSP. The temporary remission after the March laparoscopy could be explained by general anesthesia transiently resetting sensitized neural pathways (a recognized phenomenon). However, the clean episodic pattern with full remission and full return is somewhat atypical for pure central sensitization, which more commonly presents as continuous pain with fluctuating intensity rather than discrete on/off episodes.

### 9.3 Post-Surgical Nerve Entrapment or Neuroma

**Estimated Probability: MEDIUM-HIGH**

**Confirmatory Examinations:**
- **Diagnostic nerve block** (ilioinguinal, iliohypogastric, or genitofemoral nerve) — this is the definitive test. Ultrasound-guided injection of local anesthetic at the surgical site. If pain resolves temporarily, diagnosis is confirmed
- Carnett's test (bedside test: patient tenses abdominal wall; if pain worsens, it's in the wall, not visceral)
- High-resolution ultrasound of the abdominal wall at incision sites (can sometimes visualize neuromas)
- MRI neurography of the abdominal wall (specialized imaging for nerve pathology)

### 9.4 Cecal Stump Pathology (Stump Appendicitis, Stump Leak, or Stump Granuloma)

**Estimated Probability: MEDIUM-HIGH**

- MR enterography (better soft tissue resolution than CT for stump evaluation)
- Fecal calprotectin (elevated if active mucosal inflammation at the stump)

**What It Is:**
After an appendectomy with cecal pole resection, the remaining cecum is closed with staples or sutures, creating a "stump." This stump can be the source of several post-operative complications. Stump appendicitis occurs when a residual fragment of appendiceal tissue is left behind at the base; this tissue can become inflamed just as the original appendix did, causing recurrent right-sided abdominal pain that exactly mimics the original presentation. Stump leak is a rare but serious complication where the closure fails partially, allowing bowel contents to irritate the peritoneum intermittently. Stump granuloma occurs when the body mounts a foreign-body reaction to the suture material or staples used to close the stump, creating a nodule of chronic inflammation.

All three conditions can produce chronic or episodic abdominal pain that is difficult to diagnose because the appendix is assumed to have been definitively removed. Stump appendicitis in particular is underdiagnosed — it has a reported incidence of 1 in 50,000 appendectomies, but this figure likely underestimates the true rate because clinicians often do not consider it. ### 9.5 Visceral Hypersensitivity / Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome

**Estimated Probability: MEDIUM**

**Confirmatory Examinations:**
- Rectal barostat testing (measures visceral pain thresholds — the gold standard for visceral hypersensitivity, but not widely available)
- Lactulose breath test or visceral sensitivity assessment during colonoscopy (balloon distension test)
- Rome IV criteria assessment (clinical diagnosis)
- Response to neuromodulators: tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline), SNRIs (duloxetine), or pregabalin — if these help, it supports the diagnosis
- Exclusion of other causes (diagnosis of exclusion)
- Psychological assessment for central amplification factors (not to dismiss as "psychosomatic," but as part of comprehensive evaluation)

**What It Is:**
Visceral hypersensitivity is a condition in which the nerves supplying the internal organs (viscera) become abnormally sensitive, causing normal bodily functions — such as intestinal contractions, gas movement, or slight distension — to be perceived as painful. It is the leading proposed mechanism behind functional gastrointestinal disorders such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) and functional abdominal pain syndrome, though it can also develop after surgery, infection, or prolonged inflammation. The key concept is that the organs themselves are structurally normal, but the nervous system processing signals from those organs is calibrated to interpret normal input as noxious.

Visceral hypersensitivity can be triggered by a prior inflammatory event (such as appendicitis or surgery) and then persist long after the inflammation resolves — a phenomenon called "post-inflammatory visceral hypersensitivity." It is mediated by changes in both peripheral sensory neurons (in the gut wall) and central processing (in the spinal cord and brain). The condition can produce diffuse abdominal pain that is difficult to localize, which explains spread beyond the original surgical site. Unlike central sensitization (which also amplifies somatic/skin pain), visceral hypersensitivity specifically amplifies signals from the internal organs. The distinction matters because treatment approaches differ.

**Why It Fits This Case:**
Anna had a prolonged inflammatory stimulus (months of appendicitis followed by complicated surgery), which is a known trigger for post-inflammatory visceral hypersensitivity. Her digestion is "largely normal" (arguing against structural bowel disease) yet she has severe abdominal pain — this dissociation between symptoms and objective findings is characteristic of visceral hypersensitivity. The spread of pain to multiple abdominal regions (midline, upper right quadrant, pelvic) despite localized surgery is consistent. The multi-day fast not changing symptoms is also consistent, as the pain is not driven by food/digestion per se but by abnormal nerve signaling. However, the clean 2–3 week remissions are harder to explain with this diagnosis alone, as visceral hypersensitivity tends to fluctuate rather than fully remit and return.

### 9.6 Suture Granuloma or Foreign Body Reaction

**What It Is:**
A suture granuloma is a localized inflammatory reaction that the body mounts against foreign suture material left in place after surgery. ### 9.7 Adenomyosis

**Estimated Probability: MEDIUM**

**Confirmatory Examinations:**

**What It Is:**

Adenomyosis primarily affects women of reproductive age, particularly in the 30s and 40s. It can cause chronic pelvic pain, heavy menstrual bleeding, and diffuse lower abdominal pain. ### 9.8 Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS)

**Estimated Probability: MEDIUM-LOW**

**Confirmatory Examinations:**

- Cromolyn sodium trial (mast cell stabilizer)

**What It Is:**
Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS) is a condition in which mast cells — immune cells found throughout the body, especially in the gut, skin, and respiratory tract — release their chemical mediators (histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, tryptase, etc.) excessively or inappropriately. This "degranulation" can cause a wide range of symptoms across multiple organ systems, including abdominal pain and cramping, flushing, itching, diarrhea, nausea, brain fog, fatigue, and tachycardia. MCAS is distinct from mastocytosis (in which there are too many mast cells) — in MCAS, the number of mast cells is normal but they are hyperreactive.

MCAS can be triggered by surgery, infection, physical stress, or other immune activations, and can present as chronic, episodic symptoms that wax and wane without a clear pattern. The abdominal pain from MCAS is caused by mast cell degranulation in the gut wall, leading to local inflammation, visceral nerve activation, and smooth muscle spasm. Diagnosis requires demonstrating (1) episodic symptoms consistent with mast cell mediator release, (2) elevated mast cell mediators in blood or urine during a symptomatic episode, and (3) response to mast cell-directed therapy. It is frequently underdiagnosed because the lab testing must be done during a flare and requires specialized handling.

### 9.14 Small Fiber Neuropathy (SFN)

**Estimated Probability: LOW-MEDIUM**

**Confirmatory Examinations:**

**What It Is:**
Small fiber neuropathy (SFN) is a condition affecting the small-diameter nerve fibers — the A-delta and C fibers that carry pain, temperature, and autonomic signals. Unlike large fiber neuropathies (which affect motor function and are detected by standard nerve conduction studies), SFN specifically damages the thin nerve fibers responsible for pain perception. - **Strongyloides serology (IgG ELISA)** — most sensitive test for chronic Strongyloides; standard stool O&P misses it in up to 70% of cases
- Stool ova and parasites (O&P) examination — at least 3 specimens on separate days (sensitivity improves with multiple samples)
- Stool PCR panel for parasites (more sensitive than microscopy for many organisms)
- Entamoeba histolytica serology (IgG) and stool antigen test

- Duodenal aspirate or string test (Enterotest) — for Strongyloides larvae if serology is equivocal
- Liver ultrasound review — specifically for amoebic abscess (likely already done, but should be confirmed)

**What It Is:**
Chronic parasitic infections of the gastrointestinal tract can cause persistent or episodic abdominal pain that defies standard diagnostic workups. Strongyloides stercoralis is particularly relevant: it is a nematode (roundworm) endemic in tropical and subtropical regions, including the Gulf states, that can establish a chronic autoinfection cycle in the human host — meaning the parasite reinfects the same person internally without re-exposure, potentially persisting for decades. Chronic strongyloidiasis causes intermittent abdominal pain, often periumbilical or epigastric, that waxes and wanes as larval migration cycles fluctuate. It is notoriously difficult to diagnose: standard stool microscopy misses it in the majority of cases, and specific serology is required. Entamoeba histolytica can cause chronic intestinal amebiasis or amoebic liver abscess with right-upper-quadrant pain. Other protozoa (Giardia, Blastocystis hominis) and helminths can cause chronic abdominal symptoms.

The critical diagnostic challenge with these infections is that they are rarely considered in patients presenting in European healthcare systems, particularly in modern, well-groomed individuals. A physician in Vienna evaluating chronic post-surgical abdominal pain would not routinely order tropical disease screening unless specifically informed of the patient's travel history. If Anna's Dubai residence was not prominently communicated to her Austrian clinicians — or if they did not recognize Dubai as a potential exposure source — parasitic causes would be systematically overlooked. Standard blood work, CT, ultrasound, colonoscopy, and gastroscopy can all appear normal in chronic parasitic infection unless the pathologist is specifically looking for organisms on biopsy.

**Why It Fits This Case:**
Anna lived in Dubai (UAE) before her illness onset and relocated back to Vienna during the January–June 2025 period while managing subacute appendicitis. The Gulf region has endemic parasitic infections, and Strongyloides in particular can cause chronic, episodic abdominal pain with a waxing-waning pattern that matches the two remissions observed in this case. Critically, her initial presentation — "subacute appendicitis" requiring months of antibiotics — could represent ileocecal inflammation from a parasitic infection rather than true appendicitis. The extensive antibiotics given would not treat a parasitic cause and could worsen dysbiosis on top of it. Standard diagnostics performed in Austria (ultrasound, CT, colonoscopy, gastroscopy, blood work) would not detect most chronic parasitic infections unless specifically sought. The probability is tempered by the fact that Dubai is a modern city with good sanitation, and that the severity and character of her pain (7–9/10, disabling) would be unusually extreme for most parasitic infections. However, the combination of a parasitic infection plus post-surgical nerve sensitization from the appendectomy could amplify pain beyond what either cause alone would produce. Strongyloides serology is a simple blood test that could rule this in or out.
